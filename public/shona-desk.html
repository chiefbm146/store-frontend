Shona Sparrow: Architect of Cultural Understanding

How one Elder is transforming reconciliation from abstract concept into lived experience across British Columbia

For over thirty years, Shona Sparrow has stood at the intersection of tradition and transformation. As the lead cultural facilitator and guiding force behind Moon Tide Reconciliation, she brings the weight of ancestral knowledge and the urgency of contemporary healing to every workshop, every ceremony, every conversation.

Her approach is deceptively simple: reconciliation cannot be taught through lectures alone. It must be felt in the hands weaving cedar, experienced in the stories of the Kairos Blanket Exercise, and embodied in the sacred act of creating medicine pouches. Shona doesn't just teach about Indigenous culture—she creates portals for genuine understanding.

"People arrive with good intentions," Shona explains, her voice carrying the calm authority of decades in this work. "But intentions without experience are hollow. My role is to guide them through moments that shift something fundamental inside—moments they carry with them long after the workshop ends."

"We cannot command the tide toward reconciliation. We can only learn to navigate its currents together, toward a shared horizon."

This philosophy has taken Shona from corporate boardrooms to school gymnasiums, from intimate circles of Elders to packed auditoriums of educators. She's facilitated workshops for over 10,000 participants, trained hundreds of teachers in Indigenous pedagogy, and advised organizations on authentic engagement with Indigenous communities.

What sets her apart is the seamless integration of deep cultural knowledge with contemporary facilitation expertise. Shona holds traditional teachings passed down through generations while maintaining certifications in trauma-informed practice, cultural safety protocols, and experiential education methodologies.

Her workshops are meticulously crafted experiences. The Kairos Blanket Exercise becomes a visceral journey through colonization's impact. Cedar weaving transforms into meditation on interconnection. Orange Shirt Day commemorations evolve into collective commitments to action. Each element is intentional, culturally rooted, and psychologically impactful.

"Shona doesn't perform reconciliation," says one school district coordinator who has worked with her for five years. "She facilitates transformation. Participants don't just learn facts—they experience perspective shifts that ripple through their entire approach to Indigenous relationships."

Based between Douglas Lake and Vancouver, Shona remains deeply connected to her community while extending her influence across the province. She mentors emerging Indigenous facilitators, collaborates with Elders from diverse nations, and continuously refines her practice based on participant feedback and evolving cultural protocols.

Her vision for Moon Tide Reconciliation extends beyond individual workshops. She's building a network of cultural facilitators, developing resources for ongoing learning, and creating pathways for organizations to move from performative acknowledgment to substantive, sustained engagement with Indigenous communities.

As reconciliation conversations intensify across Canada, Shona's work represents a crucial model: Indigenous-led, experientially grounded, and uncompromisingly authentic. She doesn't soften hard truths, but she creates containers safe enough to hold them. She doesn't demand immediate solutions, but she guides participants toward meaningful action.

In Shona Sparrow's hands, reconciliation becomes what it should be: not a checkbox or a workshop series, but an ongoing relationship—complex, challenging, and ultimately transformative for all involved.
